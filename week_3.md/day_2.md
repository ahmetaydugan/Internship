## Day 2 - The Quiet Return:
On the second day of the week, I explored how the quiet academic efforts that persisted through the AI winter finally came to light, and I touched this revolution firsthand by coding my very first neural network architecture.

1.Historical Analysis: The Rebirth of Deep Learning I started the day by reading about the quiet academic revolution that began when Geoffrey Hinton reintroduced the term "deep learning" to the literature in 2006. It was inspiring to see how these ideas survived the cold, unfunded years of the AI winter. I also studied the creation of the ImageNet dataset spearheaded by Fei-Fei Li in 2009, which fundamentally proved just how critical massive, labeled datasets are to the success of modern machine learning.

2.Concept: Right Timing and the Delayed Revolution These historical milestones raised a fundamental question in my mind: If these concepts already existed in the 1980s, why did they have to wait until the 2006-2012 era to truly blossom? I realized the answer is directly tied to the sudden availability of massive data and the exponential growth of computing power. Although I will dive deeper into the hardware aspect on Thursday, understanding that brilliant algorithms can only perform miracles when paired with the right resources significantly broadened my perspective.

3.Practical Coding: Building the First MLP Architecture After my theoretical enlightenment, I moved on to practice and built my first model architecture—a simple Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP)—using PyTorch. I didn't train the model today; I strictly focused on defining the structure by stacking a few Linear layers. My main goal was to understand how input and output dimensions mathematically align and to calculate the total number of trainable parameters using the model.parameters() function. Seeing tens of thousands of parameters hidden within the small network I just wrote gave me a practical, hands-on sense of the actual "depth" in deep learning.
