## Day 4 — Victory and the Wall:
On the fourth day of my internship, I experienced both a sense of victory and hit what felt like an insurmountable wall. Today was an unforgettable day where I moved from theory to practice and personally experienced one of the biggest crises in AI history by coding it myself.

Practical Coding: Victory and the XOR Wall
Having thoroughly grasped NumPy methods and basic coding, I trained my perceptron model. I first tested my model on AND and OR logic gates, and seeing it work flawlessly gave me a great sense of victory. However, when I tried the model on the XOR gate, things changed; the model just couldn't learn and failed to work. Although I initially thought there was a bug in my code, I realized I had actually reproduced history's most famous "bug" with my own hands.

Concept: The Limits of the Perceptron 
This failure on the XOR gate was not actually my fault. In 1969, Marvin Minsky and Seymour Papert noticed exactly the same thing. A single-layer perceptron was helpless in non-linearly separable problems (like XOR). Learning the limitations of a model by personally coding it and getting an error was a much more permanent and instructive experience than just reading about it in a book.

History: The First AI Winter
After understanding why my model didn't work, I examined the historical aspect of the situation. Minsky and Papert's 1969 book "Perceptrons" mathematically proved this limitation. Combined with the Lighthill Report in 1973, faith in the field was lost, and research funding was cut off. This very problem that I experienced in a few minutes at my computer froze an entire research field for years, triggering the "First AI Winter." Walking through history while writing code was an incredible feeling.
