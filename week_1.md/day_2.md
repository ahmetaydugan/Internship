## Day 2 - Turing and the First Neuron:
Turing, the First Artificial Neuron, and NumPy Warm-up
On the second day of my internship, my focus was on bridging philosophy, biology, and mathematics before turning them into code. Today was all about understanding the roots of machine intelligence and preparing my practical toolkit for tomorrow.

1.Academic Block:  Turing's Timeless Vision As part of my reading assignment, I read the first section of Alan Turing's 1950 paper, "Computing Machinery and Intelligence." Reading this 76-year-old text made me appreciate the sheer imagination required to think about machine intelligence during an era when computers were basically giant calculators filling entire rooms. It made me realize that our ongoing obsession with evaluating AI based on how "human-like" it acts stems directly from Turing's Imitation Game. It is fascinating to see how the philosophical benchmarks set back then are still the ones we debate today.

2.Concept: The McCulloch-Pitts Neuron Alongside Turing's vision, I studied the 1943 McCulloch-Pitts artificial neuron. It is mind-blowing how early researchers managed to reduce the immense biological complexity of the human brain into a simple mathematical threshold logic of 0s and 1s. This transition from biology to math taught me a valuable lesson: sometimes, the greatest scientific breakthroughs come from extreme simplification and effective abstraction rather than trying to perfectly replicate nature.

3.Practical Coding: Connecting Math to Theory with NumPy Knowing that I will be coding a perceptron from scratch tomorrow, I spent today warming up with the NumPy library. However, I didn't just write random arrays; I connected the math back to the theory. I practiced array operations, matrix multiplication, and dot products—realizing that a dot product is exactly what happens inside an artificial neuron when inputs are multiplied by their weights. Seeing how NumPy's vectorization handles these operations instantly, saving me from writing clumsy iterative loops, makes me feel fully equipped and confident. I am ready to build my first perceptron tomorrow.
