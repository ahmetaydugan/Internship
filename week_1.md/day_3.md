## Day 3 - Perceptron is Born:

On the third day of my internship, I focused on the transition of artificial intelligence from its philosophical roots to its official emergence as a scientific discipline, while also strengthening my intuitive understanding of neural networks through visual learning.

1.Academic Block: The Dartmouth Conference and the Rise of AI As part of today’s research, I explored the historic 1956 Dartmouth Conference, widely considered the birthplace of Artificial Intelligence as a field. It was fascinating to see how pioneers formally introduced the idea that machines could simulate aspects of human intelligence. I also studied Frank Rosenblatt’s 1958 Perceptron model, which marked one of the first practical implementations of an artificial neuron. Understanding how the perceptron attempted to learn from data gave me a clearer picture of how modern machine learning evolved from these early ideas.

2.Concept: From Theory to the First Learning Machines Diving deeper into the perceptron, I realized how powerful yet simple this model is. It takes inputs, assigns weights, computes a weighted sum, and passes it through a threshold function to produce an output. This reinforced yesterday’s realization: complex intelligence can emerge from simple mathematical rules. It also helped me see the perceptron not just as theory, but as the foundation of classification systems used today.

3.Visual Learning: Building Intuition with 3Blue1Brown To strengthen my understanding, I watched the first episode of 3Blue1Brown’s “Neural Networks” series. The visual explanations made abstract concepts much clearer—especially how neurons, weights, and layers interact in a network. Seeing these ideas animated helped me move beyond formulas and actually feel how information flows through a neural network. This intuitive grasp will be crucial when I start implementing more complex models.

Today connected historical milestones with visual intuition, making me more confident in both the origins and the mechanics of neural networks. I now feel better prepared to not only code models, but to truly understand what’s happening under the hood.
